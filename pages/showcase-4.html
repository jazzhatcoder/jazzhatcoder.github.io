Research Poster:
Development of an Experimental Platform to Emulate Light Curve Analysis

A research poster presenting the design, methodology, and validation of a laboratory platform that emulates light curve analysis for asteroids and space debris. The poster combines theory, experiment, simulation, and comparative results to show how a controlled testbed can support more accurate and repeatable light curve research.

Reflection 01
Why I Chose This Piece

I selected this piece because it captures a type of communication challenge that is very different from writing a report or giving a standard presentation: condensing an entire research project into a poster that must communicate clearly at a glance and still support deeper technical discussion. This forced me to think carefully about what mattered most — the problem, the methodological pipeline, and the evidence that the platform actually works.

The poster also represents my research identity well. It presents a clear motivation for the work, identifies the current gap in validating light curve predictions using ground-based platforms, and then shows how my project addresses that gap through theoretical modelling, experimental setup, and simulation. That combination of technical depth and visual clarity is why I wanted it in the portfolio.

This piece came out of my undergraduate research in York University’s Astronautics and Robotics Laboratory. What makes it especially meaningful to me is that it transforms a complex research workflow into a format that can be understood by faculty, students, and other researchers in a short conversation, while still preserving the core technical rigor of the project.

Reflection 02
What It Shows About My Development as a Communicator

This poster shows growth in visual communication and research summarization. Instead of explaining the project only through dense text, I had to organize it into sections that a viewer could navigate quickly: introduction, background and gap, objectives, methodology, results, future work, and conclusion. That structure made the project easier to follow and forced me to prioritize the most important ideas.

It also shows stronger use of figures as arguments rather than decoration. The methodology panel uses diagrams and images of the setup, the results panel uses a light curve comparison graph and side-by-side visuals of the observed object, and the poster as a whole relies on those visuals to prove that the experimental platform produces meaningful results. That is a different communication skill from writing, because the viewer must be able to understand the logic of the work even before hearing a verbal explanation.

My biggest communication decision was choosing to emphasize the comparison between the lab-produced light curve and the Unreal Engine simulation. That comparison gave the poster a clear technical takeaway: the platform is not only conceptually interesting, but also validated through agreement between experimental and simulated outputs. It taught me that research communication becomes stronger when the central evidence is made immediately visible.

Reflection 03
Did It Push Me Out of My Comfort Zone?

Yes, because poster communication demands a different kind of precision. In a report, I can guide the reader step by step. In a poster, people may begin anywhere, skip sections, or ask questions immediately. That meant I had to make every section legible on its own while still keeping the full poster coherent.

The most difficult part was condensing the methodology without oversimplifying it. The project includes theoretical inputs, experimental imaging steps, and simulation work, and there was a real risk that trying to show everything would make the poster crowded. I had to accept that the poster’s job was not to contain every detail, but to communicate the logic of the system clearly enough that the audience could understand the contribution and ask the right follow-up questions.

A specific challenge was explaining the differences between the lab and simulated light curves in a concise way. The poster had to acknowledge amplitude differences, phase offset, and the role of background light and rendering effects without becoming too text-heavy. That balancing act pushed me to become more selective and intentional in how I explained results.

Reflection 04
Is This My Best Work? Why or Why Not?

This is one of my strongest pieces in terms of visual technical communication. It is not my most detailed piece of writing, but it is one of the clearest examples of how I can translate research into a form that is accessible, structured, and discussion-ready. The poster succeeds because it gives the audience a clear research problem, a visible methodology, and a results section that directly supports the conclusion.

If I revised it now, I would make a few sections slightly less text-dense and enlarge certain visual elements so that the technical comparisons are even easier to read from a distance. I would also consider making the main validation takeaway more prominent near the center of the poster, so the key message is even more immediate to someone walking by.

What makes this piece especially valuable is that it reflects a realistic research communication setting. A good poster is not just a static design; it is a tool for conversation, explanation, and feedback. This one shows that I can not only do technical research, but also present it in a way that invites engagement and supports professional discussion.
