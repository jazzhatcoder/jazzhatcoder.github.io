Part 01

Reflections on ENG2003

I entered ENG2003 with the assumption that communication was a secondary competency for an engineer — something you developed alongside your technical skills, not something that required its own dedicated study. That assumption did not survive the first three weeks of the course. What I discovered was that I had developed a sophisticated set of communication habits over years of academic writing, and that most of those habits actively worked against clear technical communication.

What the Course Required

ENG2003 operates from a simple premise that turns out to be surprisingly difficult to internalise: the purpose of technical writing is not to demonstrate your knowledge of a subject, but to produce a specific effect in a specific reader. That reorientation — from self-expression to audience service — sounds obvious in the abstract, but it cuts against the incentive structure of most academic writing, where demonstrating mastery is the explicit goal.

"The clearest technical writing is not simplified — it is precisely calibrated to the reader. Simplicity and rigour are not opposites; calibration is the skill that reconciles them."

The Three Assignments

The feasibility report, the technical memo, and the oral presentation each isolated a different communication problem. The report asked me to manage complexity across a long document and navigate a significant expertise gap with my audience. The memo asked me to produce maximum impact with minimum length — a compression problem as much as a writing problem. The presentation asked me to deliver an argument without the safety net of the written word, in real time, with immediate visible feedback from the audience.

Part 02

Growth as a Communicator

Growth as a communicator is not a linear process. Looking back across my engineering education, I can identify moments of significant forward movement — and moments where I reverted to old habits under pressure. ENG2003 did not transform my communication in a single semester; what it did was give me an explicit framework for diagnosing what is wrong when a piece of communication is not working, and a set of tools for fixing it.

The Gap Between Technical Competence and Communication Clarity

The hardest thing to accept about communication growth is that it requires a different kind of intelligence than technical problem-solving. Debugging a control algorithm requires that you understand the system more deeply. Revising a technical document often requires that you step back from your understanding and model someone else's — someone who lacks the context you have built up over months or years of work on a problem.

"Engineering communication is not a soft skill grafted onto technical work. It is a design problem: the audience is the system, and clarity is the performance requirement."

Communication in Engineering Teams

Where I Am Now and Where I Want to Go

I am a significantly more deliberate communicator than I was when I started this programme. I understand my own default tendencies — toward density, toward data-first structure, toward technical precision at the cost of accessibility — and I have developed habits for counteracting them. That awareness does not eliminate the tendencies; it means I catch them earlier and have techniques for addressing them.
