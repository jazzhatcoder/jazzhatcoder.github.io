Cover Letter:
Junior Engineer Application to IMP Aerospace & Defence

Reflection 01
Why I Chose This Piece

I chose this cover letter because it captures how I present myself when the audience is not a professor, but a real engineering employer evaluating whether I can contribute to a safety-critical aerospace environment. Unlike more academic assignments, this piece demanded that every sentence justify my relevance to the role, from research experience and technical analysis to teamwork and professional accountability.

What made it stand out for me was how directly it connected my background to a specific opportunity. Rather than describing my experience in general terms, I had to explain why my work in York University’s ASTRO Lab, my experience with structured technical documentation, and my multidisciplinary collaboration in robotics made me a strong fit for IMP Aerospace & Defence’s Aircraft Structural Integrity Programs.

This was written as an ENG2003 application-focused piece, and I was drawn to the role because it sits at the intersection of aerospace engineering, documentation, and real operational responsibility. The emphasis on aircraft integrity, safety, and mission readiness made the assignment feel concrete and meaningful, since it required me to connect my skills to work where engineering decisions have direct consequences.

Reflection 02
What It Shows About My Development as a Communicator

This letter shows growth in audience awareness, tone control, and selective evidence. The audience was a hiring committee, so the goal was not to explain every technical detail of my research, but to identify the parts most relevant to the position: traceable engineering work, defensible analysis, structured testing logic, technical documentation, and collaboration across disciplines.

It also demonstrates stronger professional framing. Instead of listing achievements, I had to build a clear argument: I understand what IMP values, I have experience that aligns with those needs, and I approach engineering work with accountability, verification, and continuous improvement. That required a more disciplined structure than earlier drafts of my writing, where I often focused too heavily on describing the work rather than explaining why it mattered to the reader.

A specific improvement was learning to translate my ASTRO Lab research into employer-relevant language. Rather than focusing only on the technical complexity of the project, I reframed it around measurable engineering models, data processing, technical deliverables, and documented workflows. That shift taught me that effective communication is not just about accuracy, but about choosing the form of accuracy that best serves the audience.

Reflection 03
Did It Push Me Out of My Comfort Zone?

Yes, because writing a cover letter is more exposed than writing a technical report. In a report, the strength of the work can often speak through calculations, evidence, and structure. In a cover letter, I had to make a direct case for myself as a candidate, which meant deciding how to sound confident without overstating my experience and how to sound professional without losing my own voice.

The biggest challenge was compression. I had to fit research, technical documentation, multidisciplinary teamwork, and my motivation for the role into a single page while still making the letter feel specific and credible. That forced me to think much more carefully about hierarchy: what the employer needs to know first, what can be implied, and what details are valuable enough to survive the page limit.

A specific discomfort was simplifying my research experience into language that would make sense in an aircraft integrity context. I spent time working through how to describe experimental aerospace systems, data analysis, and structured testing logic in a way that sounded relevant to IMP’s work without becoming vague. That challenge pushed me to connect different engineering contexts more deliberately and made my writing more adaptable.

Reflection 04
Is This My Best Work? Why or Why Not?

It is one of my stronger professional writing pieces because it is concise, tailored, and clearly grounded in the role. The letter makes a focused argument, ties my experience directly to the employer’s needs, and keeps the tone professional throughout. I think its strongest quality is that it stays specific: it names the ASIP role, references IMP’s culture and fleets, and shows how my research and teamwork experience align with the position.

At the same time, I do not think it is flawless. If I revised it now, I would strengthen the opening even further by making the value proposition more immediate and maybe sharpening one or two sentences so they sound slightly less explanatory and more decisive. I would also look for one place where I could make the connection between my research and aircraft structural integrity even more concrete.

What I like most about this piece is that it feels honest. It does not try to sound more experienced than I am, but it still presents me as someone who can learn quickly, work carefully, and contribute meaningfully. That balance is something I have had to develop over time, and this letter reflects that progress well.
