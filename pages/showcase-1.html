Technical Feasibility Report:
On-Orbit Servicing Architecture

A technical feasibility report evaluating competing robotic architectures for geostationary satellite servicing, written for a non-specialist engineering decision-maker audience and submitted as a major deliverable in ENG2003.

Reflection 01
Why I Chose This Piece

I selected this feasibility report because it marks the first time I was asked to translate genuinely complex engineering analysis into language accessible to a decision-maker without a systems engineering background. The brief required evaluating two competing robotic servicing architectures — a free-flyer platform versus a hosted payload approach — and committing to a recommendation with supporting evidence, without assuming the reader could interpret orbital mechanics or rendezvous dynamics.

That constraint made the writing problem harder than the engineering problem. It forced me to think rigorously about what my audience needed versus what I simply found technically interesting — a distinction I had rarely had to make before ENG2003. This piece is where I first understood that technical writing is a form of engineering in itself: a problem of structure, hierarchy, and audience modelling.

Reflection 02
What It Shows About My Development as a Communicator

This report demonstrates measurable growth in audience awareness and document architecture. Early drafts were dense with orbital mechanics terminology and assumed familiarity with GEO station-keeping that most readers outside the astrodynamics community would not have. Peer feedback during ENG2003 workshops revealed how consistently I confused technical accuracy with communicative effectiveness.

The final version shows a more deliberate approach: a plain-language executive summary leading with the recommendation, structured body sections that allow non-linear reading, and technical appendices that remove the burden from the main argument without losing depth. The hierarchy — recommendation first, reasoning second, supporting data third — is a pattern I now apply by default in any technical document.

Reflection 03
Did It Push Me Out of My Comfort Zone?

Yes — primarily because it required committing to a recommendation when the honest engineering answer is always "it depends." My instinct is to present trade-offs faithfully and let the reader decide. Writing a recommendation-first feasibility report forces you to override that instinct and take a position, then defend it clearly. Learning to hedge appropriately — acknowledging assumptions without undermining the argument — was something I had to practise across multiple drafts.

I was also uncomfortable with simplification. Stripping orbital mechanics to the level required for a non-specialist reader felt imprecise in ways that made me uneasy. Over time I came to see this not as a compromise but as a different kind of precision — the precision of choosing the right level of abstraction for the audience, rather than the right level of mathematical detail.

Reflection 04
Is This My Best Work? Why or Why Not?

It is among my stronger pieces from ENG2003, but not my best. The document architecture is solid and the executive summary is well-executed. Where it falls short is in visual communication. I used a single comparison table where a simple systems diagram would have communicated the architectural trade-off far more efficiently. The table requires the reader to construct a mental model that a well-designed figure would have provided immediately.

If I revised this document today, I would treat visual communication as a first-class argument — not illustration, but parallel reasoning. I now understand that in technical documents, a good figure does work that three paragraphs cannot. That insight came directly from producing this piece, but arrived too late to be applied to it. Showcase 3 reflects that lesson more fully.
