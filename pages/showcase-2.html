Group Presentation:
Hybrid Energy Storage Systems (HESS)

A collaborative ENG2003 presentation explaining why energy storage is essential for renewable energy deployment and how hybrid energy storage systems can address the limitations of individual storage technologies. My section introduced the core question of how renewable energy can be stored effectively and outlined the main categories of energy storage before the presentation moved into HESS design, implementation, and future directions.

Reflection 01
Why I Chose This Piece

I chose this presentation because it shows a different side of my communication skills than my written ENG2003 work. Instead of building an argument through a report or letter, I had to communicate technical ideas clearly through live explanation and visual structure, while also making sure my section fit smoothly into a shared group narrative.

My portion of the presentation focused on the question shown on page 3, “How can we Effectively Store Renewable Energy?”, and on page 4, where I introduced the four major categories of energy storage: mechanical, electrochemical, electrical, and thermal energy storage. That made my section foundational to the rest of the deck, because later slides build on those categories to explain why hybrid systems are needed and how they work.

This topic felt personally relevant because it connected technical explanation to a broader sustainability challenge. The presentation begins by showing why renewable energy needs storage at all on page 2, then moves toward HESS as a system-level solution on pages 6 through 11. I liked this piece because it required me to communicate both technical content and larger engineering significance.

Reflection 02
What It Shows About My Development as a Communicator

This presentation shows growth in my ability to simplify information without making it superficial. In my section, I had to introduce several storage technologies quickly and clearly, making sure the audience understood the distinctions between them and why no single one fully solves the renewable energy storage problem. That required me to focus on the most important ideas rather than trying to say everything I knew.

It also reflects stronger visual communication. The deck uses short titles, concise bullet points, and large images rather than dense text, which meant I had to treat the slides as prompts for explanation rather than as a script. That is a different communication skill from writing, because it depends on pacing, emphasis, and how well the spoken explanation connects the slides together.

A specific communication change for me was learning to let the slide structure do some of the work. Instead of over-explaining every point, I used the organization of the deck itself — from the need for storage, to storage types, to limitations, to HESS and its implementation — as part of the argument. That made the presentation feel more coherent and taught me how much clarity can come from sequence and layout.

Reflection 03
Did It Push Me Out of My Comfort Zone?

Yes. Live presentation always feels less controlled than written communication, especially when the material is technical and the audience has to follow it in real time. I had to be concise, clear, and well-paced without relying on detailed text in front of me, which made preparation and familiarity with the material much more important.

The group format added another layer of challenge. My section had to stand on its own, but it also had to prepare the audience for what came after: the limitations of individual systems on page 5, the introduction of hybrid energy storage systems on page 6, and the explanation of how HESS works on pages 7 and 8. That made transitions and coordination more important than they usually are in solo work.

The most difficult part was making sure the explanation of storage types on page 4 stayed simple enough for the audience to absorb quickly, while still being technically useful for the rest of the presentation. That pushed me to become more selective in how I explained engineering ideas and reminded me that clarity often depends on what you choose to leave out.

Reflection 04
Is This My Best Work? Why or Why Not?

This is not my strongest piece in terms of technical depth, but it is one of the strongest examples of my communication growth in a collaborative setting. Its main strength is that it presents technical material in a way that is structured, accessible, and well integrated into a broader team argument. It shows that I can contribute meaningfully to a group presentation while keeping my part clear and useful to the rest of the audience.

If I revised it now, I would make the transitions between slides even sharper and probably reduce a little more text from some later slides so the visuals carry more of the message. I would also look at how the conclusion on page 11 could connect back more directly to the early storage-type slides, so the full argument feels even more circular and complete.

What I value most about this piece is that it demonstrates communication in context. It is not just about having correct content; it is about helping an audience follow a multi-speaker technical argument from beginning to end. That is a skill I expect will matter a great deal in engineering team environments.
