Reflection 01
Why I Chose This Piece

I chose this presentation because it shows how I communicate technical ideas in a live, collaborative format rather than only through written work. My section focused on a foundational question — how renewable energy can be stored effectively — and introduced four major categories of storage before the presentation moved into the case for hybrid energy storage systems.

The piece also represents a different kind of communication challenge from my written ENG2003 work. In a group presentation, my section had to be clear on its own while also setting up the logic of the rest of the deck, including why HESS is needed, how it works, where it has already been implemented, and what limitations still remain.

This made the assignment personally relevant to my engineering interests because it required me to explain a broad technical problem clearly, connect my part to the larger team argument, and contribute to a presentation about a technology that has direct relevance to sustainability, energy systems, and real-world engineering decision-making.

Reflection 02
What It Shows About My Development as a Communicator

This presentation shows growth in my ability to simplify technical content without making it shallow. In my portion of the deck, I had to explain the main categories of energy storage clearly and quickly, using only the information most important for the audience to follow the later discussion on hybrid systems. That required stronger verbal framing and better pacing than I usually need in written assignments.

It also demonstrates development in visual communication. Because the slides relied on titles, short bullet points, and strong images rather than dense text, I had to treat the visuals as support for the spoken explanation rather than as a script. That forced me to know the material well enough to speak through it rather than read from it.

The biggest shift in my approach was learning to use each slide as a cue rather than a paragraph. Earlier drafts of my presentation style tend to depend too heavily on text, but this deck worked better when I treated the slide title and a few core points as anchors, then explained the ideas in my own words. That change made my communication more natural and more audience-focused.

Reflection 03
Did It Push Me Out of My Comfort Zone?

Yes. Presenting technical material live always feels less controlled than writing it down, and this piece pushed me to be concise in real time. I had to explain several storage technologies clearly, transition smoothly into the broader HESS argument, and do it in a way that fit the tone and pacing of a team presentation.

The collaborative format also added pressure because my section affected everyone else's. If my explanation of the storage problem or the different storage categories was unclear, the later slides about hybrid systems, implementation, and limitations would have been harder for the audience to follow. That made me think much more carefully about structure and transitions.

The question I prepared for most carefully was why hybrid systems are necessary at all if single storage technologies like lithium-ion batteries or pumped hydro already exist. The best answer came from connecting the slides logically: individual technologies each have clear limitations, while HESS is presented as a way to combine strengths and reduce weaknesses. Preparing for that kind of question pushed me to understand the full presentation, not just my own section.

Reflection 04
Is This My Best Work? Why or Why Not?

This is not my strongest piece in terms of technical depth, but it is one of the most valuable pieces in terms of communication growth. Its strength is clarity and teamwork: it shows that I can contribute to a shared presentation, explain technical concepts accessibly, and help build a coherent argument across multiple speakers.

If I revised it now, I would strengthen the transition between my section and the later HESS-specific slides even more. I would also refine the visual hierarchy so that the key takeaway from each slide is even more immediate, especially in the early part of the presentation where the audience is still building context.

I do not currently have a recording to review, but if one were available, I would use it to evaluate pacing, slide timing, and how effectively I used the visuals to support rather than repeat the spoken content. That kind of review would make it easier to identify habits that are harder to notice while presenting live.
