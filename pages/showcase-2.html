Technical Memo:
Autonomous Inspection Protocol for LEO Assets

An internal technical memorandum recommending adoption of a standardised autonomous inspection protocol for low Earth orbit satellite platforms, addressed to a senior engineering team. Produced as part of ENG2003.

Reflection 01
Why I Chose This Piece

I selected this memo because it represents a fundamentally different communication challenge from the feasibility report: where that document had to bridge an expertise gap, this memo had to communicate urgency and clarity to an audience of technical peers. The constraint here was not simplification but precision — every claim had to be supportable and every recommendation had to be operationally actionable.

The memo format also forced economy. With a strict page limit, I could not rely on volume to demonstrate thoroughness. Each sentence had to carry weight. This is the piece where I first practised writing tight — cutting words without cutting meaning.

Reflection 02
What It Shows About My Development as a Communicator

This memo demonstrates growing command of professional register and concision. The memo format requires a particular institutional voice — authoritative but collaborative, analytical but action-oriented. Finding that register took several drafts and considerable feedback.

It also shows development in the skill of subordinating supporting evidence to the recommendation rather than leading with data. Engineers tend toward data-first writing because analysis is where the work happens. But readers — even technical readers — need the argument before the evidence. This memo reflects a conscious reversal of my default writing pattern.

Reflection 03
Did It Push Me Out of My Comfort Zone?

The tone was the challenge. Writing to a senior engineering team as a student required navigating a professional authority gap that I was acutely aware of. The memo had to be confident and recommendation-driven without being presumptuous. That balance — substantiated confidence — is one I still work on.

The page limit was also genuinely difficult. My first draft was nearly double the required length. Cutting it in half without losing the essential argument required accepting that some of my supporting analysis, while technically correct, was not necessary for the reader to reach the conclusion I was recommending.

Reflection 04
Is This My Best Work? Why or Why Not?

This memo is tighter and more professionally confident than Showcase 1. The economy of the format suits me — I find that constraints tend to produce better writing, and the page limit forced decisions that improved the final document. The opening paragraph in particular is among the best I produced in ENG2003.

What it lacks is depth in the risk section. I recommended the protocol adoption without fully addressing the failure modes and operational risks that a senior engineering team would immediately identify. If I revised this, I would add a brief risk table — not as a hedge, but as evidence of rigorous thinking. An incomplete risk analysis reads as overconfidence in any technical communication context.
