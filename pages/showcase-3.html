Reflection 01
Why I Chose This Piece

Oral presentation is my least-practised communication mode — I am significantly more comfortable with structured written argumentation than with live delivery to an audience. I selected this piece precisely because it marks the most visible growth I achieved in ENG2003: I entered the course treating presentation as something to get through; I left it treating presentation as a distinct skill worth developing intentionally.

The topic also stretched my technical communication in a specific direction: making a commercial argument. The engineering case for robotic refuelling is straightforward; building the economic and strategic case for a general audience is a different problem entirely. This piece required integrating business logic into technical communication — a combination I had not previously attempted.

Reflection 02
What It Shows About My Development as a Communicator

This presentation demonstrates the most marked improvement of any piece in the showcase: between my first practice delivery and the final presentation, every measurable dimension of the performance improved. Pacing, eye contact, handling silence before key points, using visuals as support rather than a crutch — all of these were deliberate outcomes of feedback and rehearsal.

The slide design also reflects growth in visual communication. Earlier slide drafts were text-heavy and functioned as speaker notes rather than audience aids. The final deck uses data visualisations and diagrams as the primary argument, with minimal text. That shift — from slides as transcripts to slides as visual reasoning — is one of the most significant communication developments I experienced in this course.

Reflection 03
Did It Push Me Out of My Comfort Zone?

Significantly. Presenting technical material in front of an audience exposes every failure of clarity in a way that written work does not. A dense paragraph in a report can be reread; a confusing explanation in a presentation lands in real time, and the audience's reaction is immediate and visible. That pressure is uncomfortable, but it is also the most efficient feedback mechanism for identifying where your understanding of your own material has gaps.

The Q&A portion was the most challenging component. Anticipating the range of technical and non-technical questions and preparing substantive answers required a depth of preparation that went significantly beyond the content of the slides themselves. I prepared a 9-slide appendix of supporting material that I never displayed but that fundamentally changed how confidently I could answer questions.

Reflection 04
Is This My Best Work? Why or Why Not?

This is my best piece in the showcase in terms of personal growth, though whether it represents my best work depends on the metric. As a document, Showcase 1 is stronger. As a performance and communication event, this presentation represents the largest improvement from where I started.

What I would change: the opening two minutes. I began with context-setting that delayed the core argument unnecessarily. I have since learned that in a time-constrained presentation, the first minute should establish stakes rather than background. The audience will follow you into the context once they understand why it matters — but not before.
