Reflection 01
Why I Chose This Piece

I chose this report because it is one of the most complete examples of how I think as both a space engineering student and a technical communicator. Rather than focusing on a narrow subsystem, it asks a broader engineering question: whether space-based solar power can realistically contribute to a sustainable, low-carbon energy future. That made it a strong showcase piece because it required me to connect technical detail, systems thinking, and argument structure in one document.

The topic also reflects my interests well. Space-based solar power sits at the intersection of space systems, energy infrastructure, and long-term sustainability, which meant I was not just summarizing a technology but evaluating it as a “system of systems” involving orbital power generation, wireless power transmission, thermal control, rectennas, and terrestrial grid integration. I wanted to include this piece because it shows that I can engage with ambitious engineering concepts without treating them uncritically.

What made the assignment especially meaningful was that the report had to stay balanced. The document argues that SBSP is physically plausible and increasingly supported by subsystem demonstrations, but it also makes clear that cost competitiveness is not yet demonstrated under conservative assumptions. That tension between technical promise and practical limitation is exactly what made the writing interesting.

Reflection 02
What It Shows About My Development as a Communicator

This report shows growth in document architecture and evidence-driven writing. I organized the piece so that the reader moves from the large-scale energy challenge, into the technical explanation of how SBSP works, and then into a discussion that weighs strengths, limitations, and feasibility. That structure helped me present a complex concept in a way that stays readable and cumulative rather than fragmented.

It also demonstrates stronger use of quantitative evidence and visual support. Instead of describing the concept only in words, I used figures, tables, and sectioned technical explanations to show how the system works, what controls feasibility, and where the main uncertainties lie. The report’s appendix and main-body visuals strengthened the professionalism of the piece and made it easier to connect the argument to concrete engineering evidence.

A specific sign of growth came from responding to peer feedback. I revised the report by moving important tables into the main body, adding the cited figures, and making technical explanations more accessible without losing precision. That revision process taught me that strong technical communication is not only about having good information, but also about placing that information where it does the most work for the reader.

Reflection 03
Did It Push Me Out of My Comfort Zone?

Yes, because the report required me to work across technical domains rather than staying within one familiar area. To explain SBSP properly, I had to write about orbital conditions, photovoltaic conversion, microwave transmission, beam control, rectennas, cost modelling, emissions, and regulatory safety considerations in one coherent narrative. That kind of breadth is difficult because every section has to be accurate, but the document still has to feel unified.

The hardest part was writing about a technology that is easy to oversell. SBSP is exciting, and it has clear physical advantages, but the report could not become advocacy. I had to explain why the concept is promising while also being honest about the fact that launch logistics, in-space assembly, scale, and economics remain major barriers. That required a more disciplined tone than simply arguing that the idea is innovative.

The specific challenge that pushed me most was explaining how a technology can be technically plausible and environmentally attractive, yet still not be economically credible under baseline assumptions. Writing that distinction clearly forced me to think more carefully about feasibility as an engineering judgment rather than a yes-or-no conclusion.

Reflection 04
Is This My Best Work? Why or Why Not?

This is one of my strongest pieces in terms of scope, structure, and technical maturity. It combines formal report writing, quantitative evidence, visual support, and a balanced recommendation in a way that feels close to professional engineering communication. I am especially proud of the way the report moves from a global energy challenge into a detailed systems-level analysis without losing sight of the overall argument.

At the same time, I do not think it is perfect. Some sections still carry a high technical density, and although I revised the document to improve accessibility, I can still see places where a broader engineering audience might benefit from even more concise transitions or slightly simpler sentence structure. If I continued revising it, I would focus on making the strongest quantitative findings feel even more immediately interpretable.

What makes this piece valuable to me is that it shows not just the final result, but the revision process behind it. The report includes clear evidence that I responded to feedback, improved the placement of figures and tables, and refined the clarity of the writing. That willingness to revise is an important part of how I now think about technical communication.
